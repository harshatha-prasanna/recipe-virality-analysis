🍽️ Recipe Virality Analysis

Do viral recipes show different engagement timing patterns than non-viral ones?

By Harshatha Prasanna & Jazely Tong  ·  DSC 80, UC San Diego

Introduction

For this project we used the Recipes and Ratings dataset from Food.com. We were both interested in cooking and wanted to explore how recipes gain popularity over time. Our main question is whether viral recipes — defined as the top 10% of recipes by ratings received in their first 90 days — show stronger early engagement compared to non-viral recipes.

Dataset

The dataset contains two files: recipe metadata and user interactions. We merged them and computed how many days had passed since each recipe's first ever rating, which let us track early engagement over time. After cleaning, the dataset contained over 80,000 recipes and 700,000+ interactions.

Hypothesis Test

Null Hypothesis: The mean number of ratings in the first 30 days is the same for viral and non-viral recipes.

Alternative Hypothesis: Viral recipes receive more ratings in their first 30 days than non-viral recipes.

We ran a permutation test with 10,000 shuffles. The observed difference in means was 1.10 (viral: 2.10, non-viral: 1.00), and the p-value was p < 0.0001. We rejected the null hypothesis — viral recipes really do gain traction faster in their early days.

Baseline Model

Our baseline was a Logistic Regression model using three features: n_steps, n_ingredients, and ratings in the first 30 days.

Metric Non-Viral (0) Viral (1)
Precision 0.91 1.00
Recall 1.00 0.68
F1-Score 0.95 0.81

Overall accuracy was 92.6%, but the model only caught 68% of viral recipes. We plan to improve this in the final model by adding more features and switching to a Random Forest classifier.
